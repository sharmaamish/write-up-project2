What is Future Chain?

A Futures Chain displays real-time data of index or stock futures contracts across different expiries. It helps traders understand how futures participants are positioned, whether positions are being built, reduced, or rolled over, and what the market expects from the underlying index.

Why Do We Use the Future Chain?

Futures data reflects the activity of institutional and professional traders. By analyzing price, open interest, and volume together, traders can identify trend strength, direction, and conviction behind market moves.

Breaking Down the Future Data

The Futures Chain shows Nifty 50 index futures data for multiple expiries (Near, Next, and Far month) with continuous updates during market hours.

Column Explanation

Expiry

The date on which the future contract expires. Traders typically focus more on the near-month expiry due to higher liquidity.

Futures LTP

The last traded price of the futures contract. It often trades at a premium or discount compared to the spot index depending on market sentiment and cost of carry.

Change (%)

Shows how much the futures price has moved compared to the previous close. A strong positive change with rising open interest suggests aggressive long buildup.

Open Interest (OI)

Open Interest represents the total number of outstanding futures contracts. Rising OI with price increase confirms trend strength, while falling OI indicates position unwinding.

rollover cost

Rollover cost in the futures chain is the expense incurred when carrying a trading position from the current expiring month to the next month. It represents the price difference (spread) between the expiring contract and the new contract, often including transaction costs like brokerage and taxes. It reflects market conviction

key aspects

  • Cost of Carry & Demand:
    The price difference arises from the "cost of carry" (interest rates, storage, dividends) and the demand/supply of the contract.
  • Types of Roll:
    h6 A "long roll" occurs when a trader buys the next month's contract while selling the current one. A "short roll" involves selling the next month's contract while buying back the current one.
  • Example:
    If August futures are sold at ₹1,480 and September futures are bought at ₹1,490, the cost of rolling over the position is ₹10 per unit, plus associated taxes and brokerage fees.

rollover%

Rollover percentage in a futures chain measures the proportion of open interest (OI) transferred from the expiring near-month contract to the next-month contract, indicating market conviction. A high percentage suggests bullish sentiment and strong trend continuation, while low percentages may signal reduced conviction.

key aspects

  • Definition:
    The process of closing a position in the near-month contract and simultaneously opening it in the next month to maintain exposure.
  • Interpretation:
    High rollover (often compared to a 3-month average) signifies that traders are willing to pay the cost to maintain their positions, often signaling strong market trends.
  • Timing:
    Rollovers usually intensify in the days leading up to the monthly expiry
  • Cost:
    The rollover cost, or basis, is the difference between the next series price and the current series price.
  • Significance:
    It helps traders, particularly in index futures, understand market direction and avoid physical delivery of stock futures.

Change in OI

Indicates whether new positions are being added or old positions are being closed.

  • Price ↑ + OI ↑: Long buildup (bullish)
  • Price ↓ + OI ↑: Short buildup (bearish)
  • Price ↑ + OI ↓: Short covering
  • Price ↓ + OI ↓: Long unwinding

Why Futures Chain Matters More Than Options Alone

Futures represent directional bets with real money commitment. While options can reflect hedging or speculation, futures data provides a clearer picture of trend strength and institutional bias.

What is the difference between spot price and future price

Spot prices represent the current market value for immediate delivery of an asset, while future prices are agreed-upon prices for delivery at a specific future date. Spot prices reflect current supply/demand, whereas futures incorporate storage costs and interest. On expiration, both prices converge.

Key aspects

Definition & Timing:
The spot price is the current price for immediate settlement (usually within 1–2 days). The future price is a locked-in price for an asset to be delivered on a future date. Pricing Basis: Spot prices are determined by instantaneous supply and demand. Future prices are calculated based on the spot price plus the "cost of carry" (storage, insurance, interest) and market expectations.
Market Situation
: If the future price is higher than the spot price, it is called contango. If the future price is lower than the spot price, it is called backwardation.
Convergence:
As a futures contract approaches its expiration date, the futures price will move closer to the spot price, becoming equal upon expiration.
Risk & Usage:
h6 Spot trading is generally lower risk and used for immediate ownership. Futures trading involves higher risk due to leverage, often used for hedging against price changes.

Components of future chain

A futures chain (or futures matrix) is a comprehensive, organized listing of all available futures contracts for a specific underlying asset, such as a stock index, commodity, or currency. It is a critical tool for traders to analyze liquidity, price trends, and market sentiment across different expiration dates.

The key components of a futures chain display include:
1. Core Contract Details

Symbol/Name: The identifier for the underlying asset and the specific contract (e.g., "BANKNIFTY", "GOLD", "WTI Crude"). Expiry Date: The date on which the futures contract matures and trading ceases. Contract Month: Identifies the month of expiration (e.g., Nov 2019, Dec 2020).
